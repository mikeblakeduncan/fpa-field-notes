CFO Lessons: Presenting Forecasts to the Board—Lead with Impact, Not Context
by OneStream

CFOs presenting forecasts to boards should lead with critical insights and financial implications rather than building context slowly, structure presentations around clear assumptions and risk mitigation, and develop multiple scenarios (best-, base-, worst-case). Key elements include executive summary, ROI metrics with visible assumptions, top 3–5 risks with mitigation plans and kill-switch criteria, and implementation timelines with checkpoints.

WHAT YOU'LL LEARN → Start your board forecast presentation with the financial implications and ask, then support with assumptions, scenarios, and risks—respecting the board's time and preference for impact-driven storytelling.

Headcount Planning for the 2026 Budget Cycle: A Structured Four-Step Approach
by Leapsome

Running headcount planning involves assessing current workforce and gathering team needs, forecasting future demand (12–18 months ahead) by mapping business goals to staffing requirements, analyzing gaps between current and future state, and building 2–3 budget scenarios. The process requires department head meetings to identify critical roles, backfills, promotions, hiring timelines, and budget constraints.

WHAT YOU'LL LEARN → Structure your headcount planning in four steps: assess current state, forecast demand by business goal, analyze gaps, and build scenarios—allowing you to align staffing with strategic objectives and present options to leadership.

Pushing Back on Unrealistic Budget Requests: Ground Your Objection in Data and Propose Win-Win Alternatives
by Alliance Resource Group

When a business partner presents an unrealistic budget request, respond by documenting why the proposal conflicts with organizational constraints using concrete evidence and benchmarks, then understand their underlying business objective before proposing a counter-offer that addresses their core need. This shifts the discussion from personal disagreement to practical problem-solving.

WHAT YOU'LL LEARN → Ground your budget pushback in specific data and benchmarks, ask clarifying questions to understand your partner's real objective, and propose a win-win alternative rather than simply rejecting their request.
